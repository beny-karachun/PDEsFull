Day 2.5: Existence and Uniqueness (First-Order PDEs) - משפט הקיום והיחידות

Theory Focus

Existence and Uniqueness Theorem (via Characteristics):

For the first-order quasi-linear PDE $a(x,y,u)u_x + b(x,y,u)u_y = c(x,y,u)$, with initial data $u=u_0(s)$ prescribed on a curve $\Gamma$ parametrized by $s$ as $(x_0(s), y_0(s))$, a unique $C^1$ solution exists in a neighborhood of the curve $\Gamma$ provided:

  1. The functions $a, b, c$ are $C^1$ in a domain containing the initial data.
  2. The functions $x_0(s), y_0(s), u_0(s)$ are $C^1$.
  3. The **transversality condition** holds along $\Gamma$.

Key Condition (Transversality Condition):

The crucial condition derived from the Method of Characteristics is that the initial curve $\Gamma$ must **not** be tangent to the characteristic curves projecting onto the $(x,y)$ plane at any point.

Mathematically, let the initial curve projection be $\vec{\Gamma}(s) = (x_0(s), y_0(s))$. The tangent vector to the curve is $\vec{\Gamma}'(s) = (\frac{dx_0}{ds}, \frac{dy_0}{ds})$. The projection of the characteristic direction at a point on the curve is $\vec{V}(s) = (a(x_0, y_0, u_0), b(x_0, y_0, u_0))$.

The condition for existence and uniqueness is that these two vectors are not parallel. This is typically checked using the determinant:

$$ \det \begin{pmatrix} a(x_0, y_0, u_0) & b(x_0, y_0, u_0) \\ \frac{dx_0}{ds} & \frac{dy_0}{ds} \end{pmatrix} = a(x_0, y_0, u_0) \frac{dy_0}{ds} - b(x_0, y_0, u_0) \frac{dx_0}{ds} \neq 0 $$

Why? If the initial curve is characteristic (determinant is 0), the characteristic curves originating from it either overlap (potentially leading to infinitely many solutions if $u_0$ is compatible) or carry contradictory information (leading to no solution if $u_0$ is incompatible). When the condition holds (determinant $\neq 0$), the characteristics cross the initial curve transversally, ensuring that the specified $C^1$ initial data propagates uniquely into a neighborhood, yielding a unique $C^1$ solution.

Practice Questions

Question 1:

PDE: $u_x + u_y = 0$. Initial Curve $\Gamma: x_0(s)=0, y_0(s)=s$. Initial data $u_0(s) = \sin(s)$. Does a unique local $C^1$ solution exist near $\Gamma$?

Solution:

Given: $a=1, b=1, c=0$. Curve: $x_0=0, y_0=s$. Data: $u_0=\sin(s)$.

Smoothness: $a,b,c$ are $C^\infty$. $x_0, y_0, u_0$ are $C^\infty$. Conditions 1 and 2 hold.

Tangent: $\frac{dx_0}{ds}=0, \frac{dy_0}{ds}=1$.

Transversality Check: $a(x_0,y_0,u_0) \frac{dy_0}{ds} - b(x_0,y_0,u_0) \frac{dx_0}{ds} = (1)(1) - (1)(0) = 1$.

Since $1 \neq 0$ and smoothness conditions hold, the theorem guarantees a unique local $C^1$ solution exists near $\Gamma$.

Question 2:

PDE: $u_x + u_y = u$. Initial Curve $\Gamma: x_0(s)=s, y_0(s)=s$. Initial data $u_0(s)=\cos(s)$. Does a unique local $C^1$ solution exist near $\Gamma$?

Solution:

Given: $a=1, b=1, c=u$. Curve: $x_0=s, y_0=s$. Data: $u_0=\cos(s)$.

Smoothness: $a,b,c$ are $C^\infty$. $x_0, y_0, u_0$ are $C^\infty$. Conditions 1 and 2 hold.

Transversality Check: $a(x_0,y_0,u_0) \frac{dy_0}{ds} - b(x_0,y_0,u_0) \frac{dx_0}{ds} = (1)(1) - (1)(1) = 0$.

Since the transversality condition (Condition 3) fails (equals 0), the initial curve is characteristic. The theorem does **not** guarantee a unique local $C^1$ solution exists.

Question 6:

PDE: $u_t + c u_x = 0$ (c is constant). Initial Curve $\Gamma: t_0(s)=0, x_0(s)=s$. Initial data $u_0(s) = f(s)$ (assume $f$ is $C^1$). Does a unique local $C^1$ solution exist near $\Gamma$?

Variables $(x, t)$. PDE: $cu_x + 1u_t = 0$. $a=c, b=1$ (in $(x,t)$ coordinates). $c=0$.

Curve: $x_0=s, t_0=0$. Data: $u_0=f(s)$.

Tangent: $\frac{dx_0}{ds}=1, \frac{dt_0}{ds}=0$.

Transversality Check (using $t$ for $y$): $a \frac{dt_0}{ds} - b \frac{dx_0}{ds} = (c)(0) - (1)(1) = -1$.

Since $-1 \neq 0$ and smoothness conditions hold, the theorem guarantees a unique local $C^1$ solution exists near $\Gamma$.

Question 10:

PDE: $u_x - y u_y = 0$. Initial Curve $\Gamma: x_0(s)=\ln s, y_0(s)=1/s$ for $s>0$. Initial data $u_0(s) = s$. Does a unique local $C^1$ solution exist near $\Gamma$?

Solution:

Given: $a=1, b=-y, c=0$. Curve: $x_0=\ln s, y_0=1/s$ ($s>0$). Data: $u_0=s$.

Smoothness: $a,b,c$ are $C^\infty$. $x_0, y_0, u_0$ are $C^\infty$ for $s>0$. Conditions 1 and 2 hold.

Tangent: $\frac{dx_0}{ds}=1/s, \frac{dy_0}{ds}=-1/s^2$.

Evaluate $a, b$ on the curve: $a(x_0,y_0,u_0)=1, b(x_0,y_0,u_0)=-y_0=-1/s$.

Transversality Check: $a \frac{dy_0}{ds} - b \frac{dx_0}{ds} = (1)(-1/s^2) - (-1/s)(1/s) = -1/s^2 + 1/s^2 = 0$.

Since the transversality condition (Condition 3) fails, the initial curve is characteristic. The theorem does **not** guarantee a unique local $C^1$ solution exists.

(Note: $ye^x = (1/s)e^{\ln s} = (1/s)s = 1$. The characteristics are $ye^x=C$. The initial curve lies entirely on the characteristic $ye^x=1$.)
