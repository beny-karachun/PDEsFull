Laplace's Equation in Polar Coordinates

Theory Focus

Laplace's equation in polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$ is:

$$ \Delta u = u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0 $$

This is typically solved on domains like disks ($r \in [0, R]$), annuli ($r \in [R_1, R_2]$), or wedges ($\theta \in [0, \alpha]$).

Separation of Variables: Assume $u(r, \theta) = R(r)\rho(\theta)$. Substitute into the PDE:

$$ R''\rho + \frac{1}{r} R'\rho + \frac{1}{r^2} R\rho'' = 0 $$ Multiply by $r^2/(R\rho)$: $$ \frac{r^2 R'' + r R'}{R} + \frac{\rho''}{\rho} = 0 $$ Separate variables: $$ \frac{r^2 R'' + r R'}{R} = -\frac{\rho''}{\rho} = \lambda \quad (\text{separation constant}) $$ This gives two ODEs:
  1. Angular ODE: $\rho'' + \lambda \rho = 0$
  2. Radial ODE: $r^2 R'' + r R' - \lambda R = 0$ (This is a Cauchy-Euler equation)

1. Solving the Angular ODE ($\rho$)

  • Full Disk/Annulus ($r \in [0,R]$ or $r \in [R_1, R_2]$): Requires periodicity in $\theta$. The boundary conditions are $\rho(0) = \rho(2\pi)$ and $\rho'(0) = \rho'(2\pi)$. Solving $\rho'' + \lambda \rho = 0$ with these BCs yields eigenvalues $\lambda_n = n^2$ for $n=0, 1, 2, \dots$.
    • For $\lambda_0 = 0$ ($n=0$): $\rho_0(\theta) = A_0$ (constant).
    • For $\lambda_n = n^2$ ($n \ge 1$): $\rho_n(\theta) = A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta)$.
  • Wedge Domain ($\theta \in [0, \alpha]$): Typically involves Dirichlet or Neumann BCs at $\theta=0$ and $\theta=\alpha$. E.g., for $\rho(0)=0, \rho(\alpha)=0$, eigenvalues are $\lambda_n = (n\pi/\alpha)^2$ and eigenfunctions are $\rho_n(\theta) = B_n \sin(n\pi\theta/\alpha)$ for $n=1, 2, \dots$.

2. Solving the Radial ODE ($R$)

This is the Cauchy-Euler equation $r^2 R'' + r R' - \lambda R = 0$. Assume solution $R(r) = r^k$. Substitute:

$r^2 [k(k-1)r^{k-2}] + r [kr^{k-1}] - \lambda r^k = 0 \implies [k(k-1) + k - \lambda] r^k = 0 \implies (k^2 - \lambda) r^k = 0$.

We need $k^2 - \lambda = 0$, so $k = \pm\sqrt{\lambda}$.

  • Case $\lambda = \lambda_n = n^2 > 0$ ($n \ge 1$): $k = \pm n$. General solution $R_n(r) = C_n r^n + D_n r^{-n}$.
  • Case $\lambda = \lambda_0 = 0$ ($n = 0$): $k^2=0 \implies k=0$ is a repeated root. General solution $R_0(r) = C_0 r^0 + D_0 r^0 \ln r = C_0 + D_0 \ln r$.

General Solution & Boundary Conditions

Combine solutions using superposition. For example, on a **disk** $r \in [0, R]$ (requiring solution to be bounded at $r=0$):

  • Need $D_n=0$ in $R_n(r)$ to eliminate $r^{-n}$ term (unbounded as $r\to 0$).
  • Need $D_0=0$ in $R_0(r)$ to eliminate $\ln r$ term (unbounded as $r\to 0$).
  • Product solutions are $u_0(r,\theta)=A_0$ and $u_n(r,\theta)=r^n(A_n\cos(n\theta)+B_n\sin(n\theta))$.
  • General bounded solution on disk: $$ u(r, \theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty r^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$ (The $A_0/2$ is conventional for Fourier series).
  • Apply boundary condition, e.g., $u(R, \theta) = f(\theta)$: $$ f(\theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty R^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$ This is a Full Fourier Series for $f(\theta)$ on $[0, 2\pi]$ (or $[-\pi, \pi]$). The coefficients $A_n R^n$ and $B_n R^n$ are found using the standard formulas (with $L=\pi$ if using $[-\pi, \pi]$): $$ A_0 R^0 = A_0 = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) d\theta $$ $$ A_n R^n = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) \cos(n\theta) d\theta \implies A_n = \frac{1}{\pi R^n} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) \cos(n\theta) d\theta \quad (n \ge 1) $$ $$ B_n R^n = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) \sin(n\theta) d\theta \implies B_n = \frac{1}{\pi R^n} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) \sin(n\theta) d\theta \quad (n \ge 1) $$

For an **annulus** $r \in [R_1, R_2]$, both $r^n$ and $r^{-n}$ (and $\ln r$) terms are generally kept, and coefficients are determined by boundary conditions at $r=R_1$ and $r=R_2$.

Practice Questions

Question 1:

Find the general solution to $\rho'' + 4\rho = 0$.

Solution:

Characteristic equation $r^2+4=0 \implies r = \pm 2i$.

General solution $\rho(\theta) = C_1 \cos(2\theta) + C_2 \sin(2\theta)$.

Question 2:

Find the general solution to $r^2 R'' + r R' - 9 R = 0$.

Solution:

Cauchy-Euler equation. Try $R=r^k$. Indicial equation $k^2 - \lambda = 0$. Here $\lambda=9$.

$k^2 - 9 = 0 \implies k = \pm 3$.

General solution $R(r) = C_1 r^3 + C_2 r^{-3}$.

Question 3:

Find the general solution to $r^2 R'' + r R' = 0$.

Solution:

Cauchy-Euler equation with $\lambda=0$. Indicial equation $k^2 = 0 \implies k=0$ (repeated root).

General solution $R(r) = C_1 r^0 + C_2 r^0 \ln r = C_1 + C_2 \ln r$.

Question 4:

What are the eigenvalues $\lambda_n$ and eigenfunctions $\rho_n(\theta)$ for $\rho''+\lambda\rho=0$ with periodic boundary conditions $\rho(0)=\rho(2\pi), \rho'(0)=\rho'(2\pi)$?

Solution:

Case $\lambda=0$: $\rho=A\theta+B$. $\rho(0)=B$. $\rho(2\pi)=2\pi A+B$. Need $B=2\pi A+B \implies A=0$. $\rho'(\theta)=A=0$. Periodic BCs are satisfied for $A=0$. Solution $\rho_0(\theta)=B$ (constant). Eigenvalue $\lambda_0=0$.

Case $\lambda=\mu^2>0$: $\rho(\theta)=A\cos(\mu\theta)+B\sin(\mu\theta)$.

$\rho(0)=A$. $\rho(2\pi)=A\cos(2\pi\mu)+B\sin(2\pi\mu)$. Need $A = A\cos(2\pi\mu)+B\sin(2\pi\mu)$ (Eq 1).

$\rho'(\theta)=-A\mu\sin(\mu\theta)+B\mu\cos(\mu\theta)$. $\rho'(0)=B\mu$. $\rho'(2\pi)=-A\mu\sin(2\pi\mu)+B\mu\cos(2\pi\mu)$. Need $B\mu = -A\mu\sin(2\pi\mu)+B\mu\cos(2\pi\mu)$ (Eq 2).

For non-trivial solution (A,B not both zero), the determinant of the system for A,B derived from Eq1 and Eq2 must be zero. This requires $\cos(2\pi\mu)=1$ and $\sin(2\pi\mu)=0$. This occurs when $2\pi\mu = 2n\pi$, so $\mu=n$ for $n=1, 2, 3, \dots$.

Eigenvalues $\lambda_n = \mu^2 = n^2$ for $n=0, 1, 2, \dots$.

Eigenfunctions: For $\lambda_0=0$, $\rho_0=A_0$. For $\lambda_n=n^2 (n\ge 1)$, $\rho_n(\theta)=A_n\cos(n\theta)+B_n\sin(n\theta)$.

Question 5:

Write the general form of a bounded solution to Laplace's equation $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ inside a disk $r \in [0, R]$.

Solution:

Separation leads to $\lambda_n=n^2$ and $\rho_n(\theta) = A_n\cos(n\theta)+B_n\sin(n\theta)$ (for $n\ge 1$) or $A_0$ (for $n=0$).

The radial equation $r^2R''+rR' - n^2 R = 0$ has solutions $R_n(r)=C_n r^n + D_n r^{-n}$ for $n\ge 1$, and $R_0(r)=C_0 + D_0 \ln r$ for $n=0$.

For the solution $u(r,\theta)=\sum R_n(r)\rho_n(\theta)$ to be bounded at $r=0$, we must discard the terms $r^{-n}$ (for $n\ge 1$) and $\ln r$ (for $n=0$). So $D_n=0$ for all $n \ge 0$.

The bounded solution form is:

$$ u(r, \theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty r^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$

(Constants absorbed, $A_0/2$ used for convention).

Question 6:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [0, 1]$, with $u(1, \theta) = 5$.

Solution:

Use the general bounded solution form from Q5 with $R=1$.

$$ u(r, \theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty r^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$

Apply BC $u(1, \theta) = 5$.

$$ 5 = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty 1^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$ $$ 5 = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty ( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) ) $$

This is the Full Fourier Series for the constant function $f(\theta)=5$.

The only non-zero coefficient is the constant term $A_0/2$.

$A_0 = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} 5 d\theta = \frac{1}{\pi} [5\theta]_0^{2\pi} = \frac{1}{\pi}(10\pi) = 10$. So $A_0/2 = 5$.

$A_n = \frac{1}{\pi 1^n} \int_0^{2\pi} 5 \cos(n\theta) d\theta = \frac{5}{\pi} [\frac{\sin(n\theta)}{n}]_0^{2\pi} = 0$ for $n \ge 1$.

$B_n = \frac{1}{\pi 1^n} \int_0^{2\pi} 5 \sin(n\theta) d\theta = \frac{5}{\pi} [-\frac{\cos(n\theta)}{n}]_0^{2\pi} = -\frac{5}{n\pi}[\cos(2n\pi)-\cos(0)] = -\frac{5}{n\pi}[1-1]=0$ for $n \ge 1$.

All coefficients except $A_0$ are zero.

The solution is $u(r, \theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} = 5$.

Question 7:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [0, R]$, with $u(R, \theta) = \cos(3\theta)$.

Solution:

Use the general bounded solution form:

$$ u(r, \theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty r^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$

Apply BC $u(R, \theta) = \cos(3\theta)$.

$$ \cos(3\theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty R^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$

This is the Fourier Series for $\cos(3\theta)$. By matching coefficients:

The constant term $A_0/2$ is 0, so $A_0=0$.

All sine coefficients ($B_n R^n$) are 0, so $B_n=0$ for all $n$.

For the cosine terms, only the $n=3$ term is non-zero.

$R^3 A_3 = 1$. All other $A_n R^n = 0$ (for $n \neq 3$).

So $A_3 = 1/R^3$, and $A_n=0$ for $n \neq 3$.

The solution is $u(r, \theta) = r^3 (A_3 \cos(3\theta)) = r^3 (\frac{1}{R^3} \cos(3\theta))$.

$u(r, \theta) = (\frac{r}{R})^3 \cos(3\theta)$.

Question 8:

Write the general solution form for Laplace's equation in an annulus $r \in [R_1, R_2]$, assuming periodicity in $\theta$.

Solution:

Periodicity gives $\lambda_n = n^2$ and $\rho_n(\theta) = A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta)$ ($n \ge 1$), $\rho_0 = A_0$.

The radial equation $r^2R''+rR' - n^2 R = 0$ has solutions $R_n(r)=C_n r^n + D_n r^{-n}$ ($n \ge 1$) and $R_0(r)=C_0 + D_0 \ln r$.

Since $r=0$ is *not* in the domain $r \in [R_1, R_2]$ (assuming $R_1>0$), we do not need to discard the $r^{-n}$ or $\ln r$ terms based on boundedness at the origin.

Combining the product solutions $R_n(r)\rho_n(\theta)$ and using superposition:

$$ u(r, \theta) = (C_0 + D_0 \ln r) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty (C_n r^n + D_n r^{-n}) (A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta)) $$

It's conventional to absorb constants and rewrite:

$$ u(r, \theta) = (C_0 + D_0 \ln r) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left[ (A_n r^n + B_n r^{-n})\cos(n\theta) + (C_n r^n + D_n r^{-n})\sin(n\theta) \right] $$

(The constants $A_n, B_n, C_n, D_n$ here are different from the intermediate ones).

Question 9:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [1, 2]$, with $u(1, \theta) = 1$ and $u(2, \theta) = 3$.

Solution:

The boundary conditions are independent of $\theta$. This suggests the solution $u$ is also independent of $\theta$, i.e., $u=u(r)$.

If $u=u(r)$, then $u_{\theta\theta}=0$. Laplace's equation becomes $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r = 0$.

This is $(r u_r)' = 0$. Integrating gives $r u_r = C_1$.

$u_r = C_1/r$. Integrating again gives $u(r) = C_1 \ln r + C_2$.

Apply BCs:

$u(1) = C_1 \ln 1 + C_2 = C_1(0) + C_2 = C_2 = 1$.

$u(2) = C_1 \ln 2 + C_2 = C_1 \ln 2 + 1 = 3$.

$C_1 \ln 2 = 2 \implies C_1 = 2 / \ln 2$.

The solution is $u(r, \theta) = u(r) = \frac{2 \ln r}{\ln 2} + 1$.

(Alternatively, using the general annulus solution form from Q8, setting $f(\theta)=1, g(\theta)=3$. Only the $n=0$ term survives. $u(r,\theta)=C_0+D_0\ln r$. $u(1,\theta)=C_0+D_0\ln 1 = C_0 = 1$. $u(2,\theta)=C_0+D_0\ln 2 = 1+D_0\ln 2 = 3$. $D_0\ln 2 = 2 \implies D_0=2/\ln 2$. $u(r,\theta)=1+(2/\ln 2)\ln r$.)

Question 10:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [0, 1]$, with $u(1, \theta) = 4\sin(2\theta)$.

Solution:

Bounded solution on disk $R=1$. General form:

$$ u(r, \theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty r^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$

Apply BC $u(1, \theta) = 4\sin(2\theta)$.

$$ 4\sin(2\theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty 1^n \left( A_n \cos(n\theta) + B_n \sin(n\theta) \right) $$

Matching Fourier coefficients:

$A_0/2 = 0 \implies A_0=0$. $A_n=0$ for all $n$.

Only the $n=2$ sine term is non-zero. $B_2 (1^2) = 4 \implies B_2=4$. $B_n=0$ for $n \neq 2$.

The solution is $u(r, \theta) = r^2 (B_2 \sin(2\theta)) = r^2 (4 \sin(2\theta))$.

$u(r, \theta) = 4r^2 \sin(2\theta)$.

Question 11:

What is the general solution to $\rho''=0$ satisfying periodic BCs $\rho(0)=\rho(2\pi), \rho'(0)=\rho'(2\pi)$?

Solution:

General solution $\rho(\theta)=A\theta+B$. $\rho'(\theta)=A$.

$\rho(0)=B$. $\rho(2\pi)=2\pi A+B$. Need $B=2\pi A+B \implies A=0$.

$\rho'(0)=A$. $\rho'(2\pi)=A$. Need $A=A$, which is always true.

The condition requires $A=0$. So the solution is $\rho(\theta)=B$ (any constant).

Question 12:

Find the specific solution to $r^2R''+rR'=0$ that satisfies $R(1)=5, R(e)=7$.

Solution:

General solution is $R(r)=C_1+C_2\ln r$.

$R(1)=C_1+C_2\ln 1 = C_1+0 = 5 \implies C_1=5$.

$R(e)=C_1+C_2\ln e = 5+C_2(1) = 7 \implies C_2=2$.

Solution is $R(r)=5+2\ln r$.

Question 13:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [0, 2]$, with $u(2, \theta) = 1 + \cos(2\theta)$.

Solution:

Disk $R=2$. Bounded solution form: $u(r,\theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum r^n(A_n\cos n\theta + B_n\sin n\theta)$.

$u(2,\theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum 2^n(A_n\cos n\theta + B_n\sin n\theta) = 1 + \cos(2\theta)$.

Matching coefficients:

Constant term: $A_0/2 = 1 \implies A_0=2$.

$n=2$ cosine term: $2^2 A_2 = 1 \implies 4A_2=1 \implies A_2=1/4$.

All other $A_n=0$. All $B_n=0$.

$u(r,\theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + r^2(A_2\cos 2\theta) = \frac{2}{2} + r^2(\frac{1}{4}\cos 2\theta) = 1 + \frac{r^2}{4}\cos(2\theta)$.

Question 14:

Find the eigenvalues $\lambda_n$ for $\rho''+\lambda\rho=0$ with $\rho(0)=0, \rho(\pi/2)=0$.

Solution:

This is a standard Dirichlet problem on $[0, L]$ with $L=\pi/2$.

The eigenvalues are $\lambda_n = (n\pi/L)^2 = (n\pi/(\pi/2))^2 = (2n)^2 = 4n^2$ for $n=1, 2, 3, \dots$.

Question 15:

Write the general bounded solution form for Laplace's Eq. in a wedge $r \in [0, R], \theta \in [0, \alpha]$ with $u(r,0)=0, u(r,\alpha)=0$.

Solution:

Angular problem: $\rho''+\lambda\rho=0, \rho(0)=0, \rho(\alpha)=0$. Eigenvalues $\lambda_n=(n\pi/\alpha)^2$. Eigenfunctions $\rho_n(\theta)=\sin(n\pi\theta/\alpha)$, $n=1, 2, \dots$.

Radial problem: $r^2 R''+rR'-\lambda_n R=0$. Since $\lambda_n>0$, $k=\pm\sqrt{\lambda_n}=\pm n\pi/\alpha$. Solution $R_n(r)=C_n r^{n\pi/\alpha} + D_n r^{-n\pi/\alpha}$.

For boundedness at $r=0$, we need $D_n=0$.

General bounded solution form:

$$ u(r,\theta) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty C_n r^{n\pi/\alpha} \sin\left(\frac{n\pi\theta}{\alpha}\right) $$

Question 16:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [1, e]$, $u(1,\theta)=0, u(e,\theta)=\sin(2\theta)$.

Solution:

Annulus problem. General solution from Q8:

$$ u(r, \theta) = (C_0 + D_0 \ln r) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left[ (A_n r^n + B_n r^{-n})\cos(n\theta) + (C_n r^n + D_n r^{-n})\sin(n\theta) \right] $$

Apply $u(1,\theta)=0$:

$0 = (C_0+D_0\ln 1) + \sum [(A_n 1^n+B_n 1^{-n})\cos n\theta + (C_n 1^n+D_n 1^{-n})\sin n\theta]$. $0 = C_0 + \sum [(A_n+B_n)\cos n\theta + (C_n+D_n)\sin n\theta]$. This implies $C_0=0$, $A_n+B_n=0 \implies B_n=-A_n$, $C_n+D_n=0 \implies D_n=-C_n$.

Solution form becomes: $u(r,\theta) = D_0 \ln r + \sum [A_n(r^n-r^{-n})\cos n\theta + C_n(r^n-r^{-n})\sin n\theta]$.

Apply $u(e,\theta)=\sin(2\theta)$:

$\sin(2\theta) = D_0 \ln e + \sum [A_n(e^n-e^{-n})\cos n\theta + C_n(e^n-e^{-n})\sin n\theta]$. $\sin(2\theta) = D_0 + \sum [2A_n\sinh(n)\cos n\theta + 2C_n\sinh(n)\sin n\theta]$.

Matching coefficients:

$D_0=0$. $A_n=0$ for all $n$. $n=2$ sine term: $2C_2\sinh(2)=1$. $C_n=0$ for $n\neq 2$. $C_2 = 1/(2\sinh 2)$. $D_2 = -C_2 = -1/(2\sinh 2)$.

Solution: $u(r,\theta) = C_2(r^2-r^{-2})\sin(2\theta) = \frac{r^2-r^{-2}}{2\sinh 2}\sin(2\theta)$.

(Note: $r^n-r^{-n} = 2\sinh(n\ln r)$). $u(r,\theta)=\frac{\sinh(2\ln r)}{\sinh 2}\sin(2\theta)$ is equivalent.

Question 17:

What is the value of $u(0,0)$ if $u$ is harmonic in the disk $r

Solution:

By the Mean Value Property, $u(0,0)$ is the average value of $u$ on the boundary circle $r=R$.

$$ u(0,0) = \frac{1}{2\pi R} \oint_{r=R} u \, ds = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} u(R,\theta) \, d\theta = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) \, d\theta $$

This is also $\frac{A_0}{2}$ from the Fourier series solution.

Question 18:

Find the coefficient $A_0$ for the bounded solution of Laplace's equation in the disk $r<3$ with $u(3,\theta)=\theta^2$ for $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$.

Solution:

The coefficient $A_0/2$ is the average value of the boundary function.

$A_0 = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) d\theta = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \theta^2 d\theta$.

$A_0 = \frac{1}{\pi} [\frac{\theta^3}{3}]_0^{2\pi} = \frac{1}{\pi} [\frac{(2\pi)^3}{3} - 0] = \frac{8\pi^3}{3\pi} = \frac{8\pi^2}{3}$.

($A_0/2 = 4\pi^2/3$).

Question 19:

Find the coefficient $A_1$ for the problem in Q18.

Solution:

$R=3$. $A_n = \frac{1}{\pi R^n} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) \cos(n\theta) d\theta$.

$A_1 = \frac{1}{\pi (3^1)} \int_0^{2\pi} \theta^2 \cos(\theta) d\theta$.

Integrate $\int \theta^2 \cos \theta d\theta$ by parts twice.

$u=\theta^2, dv=\cos\theta d\theta \implies du=2\theta d\theta, v=\sin\theta$.

$\int = \theta^2\sin\theta - \int 2\theta\sin\theta d\theta$.

$\int \theta\sin\theta d\theta$: $u=\theta, dv=\sin\theta d\theta \implies du=d\theta, v=-\cos\theta$.

$\int = -\theta\cos\theta - \int (-\cos\theta)d\theta = -\theta\cos\theta + \sin\theta$.

Overall integral: $[\theta^2\sin\theta - 2(-\theta\cos\theta + \sin\theta)]_0^{2\pi}$.

$= [\theta^2\sin\theta + 2\theta\cos\theta - 2\sin\theta]_0^{2\pi}$.

At $2\pi$: $0 + 2(2\pi)\cos(2\pi) - 0 = 4\pi$. At $0$: $0+0-0=0$. Value is $4\pi$.

$A_1 = \frac{1}{3\pi} (4\pi) = 4/3$.

Question 20:

Find the coefficient $B_1$ for the problem in Q18.

Solution:

$R=3$. $B_n = \frac{1}{\pi R^n} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) \sin(n\theta) d\theta$.

$B_1 = \frac{1}{3\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \theta^2 \sin(\theta) d\theta$.

Integrate $\int \theta^2 \sin \theta d\theta$ by parts twice.

$u=\theta^2, dv=\sin\theta d\theta \implies du=2\theta d\theta, v=-\cos\theta$.

$\int = -\theta^2\cos\theta - \int (-\cos\theta)(2\theta d\theta) = -\theta^2\cos\theta + 2\int \theta\cos\theta d\theta$.

$\int \theta\cos\theta d\theta$: $u=\theta, dv=\cos\theta d\theta \implies du=d\theta, v=\sin\theta$.

$\int = \theta\sin\theta - \int \sin\theta d\theta = \theta\sin\theta + \cos\theta$.

Overall integral: $[-\theta^2\cos\theta + 2(\theta\sin\theta + \cos\theta)]_0^{2\pi}$.

At $2\pi$: $-(2\pi)^2\cos(2\pi) + 2(0+\cos(2\pi)) = -4\pi^2 + 2$.

At $0$: $-0 + 2(0+\cos(0)) = 2$.

Value is $(-4\pi^2+2) - 2 = -4\pi^2$.

$B_1 = \frac{1}{3\pi} (-4\pi^2) = -4\pi/3$.

Question 21:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [0, 1]$, $u(1,\theta) = \sin\theta$.

Solution:

$R=1$. $u(r,\theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum r^n(A_n \cos n\theta + B_n \sin n\theta)$. $u(1,\theta) = \frac{A_0}{2} + \sum (A_n \cos n\theta + B_n \sin n\theta) = \sin\theta$. Match coeff: $A_n=0$. $B_1 = 1$, $B_n=0$ for $n\neq 1$. $u(r,\theta) = r^1(B_1 \sin \theta) = r\sin\theta$.

Question 22:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [1, 2]$, $u(1,\theta)=\cos\theta$, $u(2,\theta)=0$.

Solution:

Annulus. $u(r,\theta) = (C_0+D_0\ln r) + \sum [(A_n r^n+B_n r^{-n})\cos n\theta + (C_n r^n+D_n r^{-n})\sin n\theta]$. BCs only involve $n=1$ cosine term. $C_0=D_0=0$. $C_n=D_n=0$. $u(r,\theta)=(A_1 r + B_1 r^{-1})\cos\theta$. $u(1,\theta)=(A_1+B_1)\cos\theta = \cos\theta \implies A_1+B_1=1$. $u(2,\theta)=(2A_1+B_1/2)\cos\theta = 0 \implies 2A_1+B_1/2=0$. $B_1=1-A_1$. $2A_1+(1-A_1)/2=0 \implies 4A_1+1-A_1=0 \implies 3A_1=-1 \implies A_1=-1/3$. $B_1=1-(-1/3)=4/3$. $u(r,\theta)=(-\frac{1}{3}r+\frac{4}{3}r^{-1})\cos\theta = \frac{4-r^2}{3r}\cos\theta$.

Question 23:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [0, R]$, $u(R,\theta)=100$.

Question 24:

Find eigenvalues $\lambda_n$ for $\rho''+\lambda\rho=0$, $\rho'(0)=0, \rho'(\pi)=0$.

Solution:

Neumann on $[0, \pi]$. $\lambda_n = (n\pi/L)^2 = (n\pi/\pi)^2=n^2$, for $n=0, 1, 2, \dots$. Eigenfunctions $\cos(nx)$.

Question 25:

Solve $r^2R''+rR'-\frac{1}{4}R=0$.

Solution:

Euler Eq. $\lambda=1/4$. $k^2-\lambda=0 \implies k^2-1/4=0 \implies k=\pm 1/2$. $R(r)=C_1 r^{1/2} + C_2 r^{-1/2}$.

Question 26:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [0, 1]$, $u(1,\theta)=f(\theta)$. What is $u(0,\theta)$?

Solution:

The value at the center is the average value on the boundary. $u(0,\theta)=u(0,0)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) d\theta = A_0/2$. It's a constant.

Question 27:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [1, \infty)$, $u(1,\theta)=\cos(2\theta)$, $u$ bounded as $r\to\infty$.

Solution:

Exterior problem. General solution (from Q8). $u(r, \theta) = (C_0 + D_0 \ln r) + \sum [(A_n r^n + B_n r^{-n})\cos(n\theta) + (C_n r^n + D_n r^{-n})\sin(n\theta)]$. For boundedness as $r\to\infty$, we need $D_0=0$, $A_n=0$, $C_n=0$ for $n\ge 1$. Form is $u(r,\theta)=C_0 + \sum [B_n r^{-n}\cos n\theta + D_n r^{-n}\sin n\theta]$. Apply $u(1,\theta)=\cos(2\theta)$. $C_0=0$. $D_n=0$. $B_2(1^{-2})=1 \implies B_2=1$. $B_n=0$ for $n\neq 2$. $u(r,\theta)=B_2 r^{-2}\cos(2\theta) = r^{-2}\cos(2\theta)$.

Question 28:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for $r \in [0, 1]$, $u(1, \theta) = \theta$ for $\theta \in [0, \pi]$, $u(1, \theta)=0$ for $\theta \in (\pi, 2\pi)$. Find $A_0$.

Solution:

$A_0 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) d\theta = \frac{1}{\pi}[\int_0^\pi \theta d\theta + \int_\pi^{2\pi} 0 d\theta]$. $A_0 = \frac{1}{\pi}[\frac{\theta^2}{2}]_0^\pi = \frac{1}{\pi}[\frac{\pi^2}{2}-0]=\pi/2$. ($u(0,0)=A_0/2=\pi/4$).

Question 29:

Solve $u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r} u_r + \frac{1}{r^2} u_{\theta\theta} = 0$ for wedge $r \in [0, 1], \theta \in [0, \pi/2]$, $u(r,0)=0, u(r,\pi/2)=0, u(1,\theta)=\sin(2\theta)$.

Solution:

Wedge $\alpha=\pi/2$. $\rho(0)=\rho(\pi/2)=0 \implies \lambda_n=(n\pi/(\pi/2))^2 = (2n)^2$. $\rho_n(\theta)=\sin(2n\theta)$, $n=1, 2, \dots$. Radial part (bounded) $R_n(r)=C_n r^{\sqrt{\lambda_n}}=C_n r^{2n}$. General solution $u(r,\theta)=\sum C_n r^{2n} \sin(2n\theta)$. BC $u(1,\theta)=\sum C_n (1)^{2n}\sin(2n\theta) = \sin(2\theta)$. Match coeff: $n=1$. $C_1=1$. $C_n=0$ for $n\neq 1$. Solution $u(r,\theta)=C_1 r^{2(1)}\sin(2(1)\theta) = r^2\sin(2\theta)$.

Question 30:

What is the relation between Laplace's equation in polar coordinates and the Cauchy-Euler ODE?

Solution:

When separation of variables $u(r,\theta)=R(r)\rho(\theta)$ is applied to Laplace's equation in polar coordinates, the resulting ordinary differential equation for the radial part $R(r)$ is the Cauchy-Euler equation: $r^2 R'' + r R' - \lambda R = 0$, where $\lambda$ is the separation constant determined by the angular part $\rho(\theta)$ and its boundary conditions.
